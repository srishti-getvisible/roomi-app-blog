Getting to know Springfield, IL

Springfield is the capital of the U.S. state of Illinois and the county seat and largest city of Sangamon County. Present-day Springfield was settled by European Americans in the late 1810s, around the time Illinois became a state.

The most famous historic resident of Springfield is Abraham Lincoln. He lived here from 1837 until 1861 when he went to the White House as President. So, some of the most popular tourist attractions you will find here are museums dedicated to the legacy of Abraham Lincoln.

Specifically, the city lies in a valley and plain near the Sangamon River. The weather in Springfield is also fairly typical for middle latitude locations. There are four distinct seasons, including hot summers and cold winters.

Springfield has at least twenty separately designated neighborhoods, though not all are incorporated with associations. University life in Springfield, IL

Springfield hosts one University – The University of Illinois at Springfield (UIS, formerly Sangamon State University), which is located on the southeast side of the city. Springfield is also home to a junior college Lincoln Land Community College, located just south of UIS. From 1875 to 1976, Springfield was also home to Concordia Theological Seminary. The seminary was moved back to its original home of Fort Wayne, Indiana, and the campus now serves as the Illinois Department of Corrections Academy.

Roads and traffic

Springfield is in central Illinois at the crossroads of Interstates 55 and 97. There’s also the I-72, which is concurrent with U.S. Route 36 from the Missouri state line to Decatur, which runs from east to west. The Sangamon Mass Transit District (SMTD) operates Springfield’s bus system. The city also lies along the historic Route 66.

Public transport

Amtrak serves Springfield daily with its Lincoln Service and Texas Eagle routes. Service consists of four Lincoln Service round-trips between Chicago and St. Louis, and one Texas Eagle round-trip between San Antonio and Chicago. Additionally, three days a week, the Eagle continues on to Los Angeles.

Springfield is also served by the following freight railroads: Canadian National Illinois, and Midland Railroad, Kansas City Southern, Norfolk Southern, and Union Pacific. Travelers can also catch Greyhound buses at a station on North Dirksen Parkway.

Cycling

Springfield and the surrounding metropolitan area have constructed bike trails and bike lanes on a number of streets. Currently, four main trails exist; two significant paved trails, the Interurban Trail and the Lost Bridge Trail serve Springfield and its suburbs of Chatham, Illinois, and Rochester, Illinois respectively. The Lost Bridge Trail has been extended further into Springfield by the Bunn to Lost Bridge Trail, which follows a stretch of Ash Street and Taylor Avenue.

Historical and Cultural Spots in Springfield, IL

Springfield has been home to a wide array of individuals, who, in one way or another, contributed to the broader American culture.

The Hoogland Center for the Arts in downtown Springfield is a centerpiece for performing arts. Before being purchased and renamed, the Hoogland Center was Springfield’s Masonic Temple. Prior to the Hoogland, the Springfield Theatre Centre was housed in the nearby Legacy Theatre. Sangamon Auditorium, located on the campus of the University of Illinois at Springfield also serves as a larger venue for musical and performing acts, both touring and local.

Springfield is also home to the annual Springfield Old Capitol Art Fair, a spring festival held annually on the third weekend of May. And more interestingly so, since 2002, Springfield has also hosted the ‘Route 66 Film Festival’, set to celebrate films routed in, based on, or taking part on the famous Route 66.

Live music performances also take place at lunchtime on weekdays at Trout Lily Cafe and on Friday evening’s at Robbie’s, which features jazz.
